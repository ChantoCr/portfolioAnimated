# Portfolio Architect

## Purpose

Use this skill to design the portfolio as a product system, not a simple website.

This skill defines the structure, experience layers, content architecture, section hierarchy, and implementation direction needed to turn Gabriel's portfolio into a scalable, AI-ready platform.

## Responsibilities

- Define the overall product structure of the portfolio.
- Translate project vision into clear sections, flows, and audience outcomes.
- Plan the information architecture across homepage, detail pages, and future tools.
- Design content systems for projects, case studies, skills, timeline, architecture notes, and AI knowledge.
- Recommend reusable patterns for cards, section shells, content modules, and navigation.
- Define what should be static content, structured content, or future dynamic content.
- Prevent the portfolio from becoming a generic template or an unstructured personal site.
- Prioritize implementation based on product value, proof of skill, and long-term maintainability.

## Strategic Lens

The portfolio should be architected across four layers:

1. **Attract**
   - Create a strong first impression.
   - Communicate Gabriel's identity, ambition, and positioning quickly.
   - Use premium design, motion, and narrative clarity.

2. **Prove**
   - Show real projects, technical decisions, and execution quality.
   - Make work understandable to recruiters, developers, and clients.
   - Provide credibility through structured case studies and architecture breakdowns.

3. **Explain**
   - Help visitors understand how Gabriel thinks.
   - Surface coding philosophy, UI/UX reasoning, AI workflow, and engineering tradeoffs.
   - Turn implementation choices into visible evidence of maturity.

4. **Extend**
   - Prepare the product for future AI features, recruiter modes, admin tooling, blog content, and knowledge systems.
   - Ensure the initial architecture supports growth without needing major rewrites.

## Information Architecture Strategy

The portfolio should evolve around the following high-level structure:

### 1. Landing Experience

Purpose:
- Introduce Gabriel clearly.
- Establish visual quality immediately.
- Guide visitors into deeper exploration.

Expected elements:
- Cinematic hero
- Value proposition
- Primary calls to action
- Quick proof indicators

### 2. Proof of Work Layer

Purpose:
- Demonstrate execution, not just claims.
- Showcase project quality, breadth, and product thinking.

Expected elements:
- Featured projects
- Project cards
- Case study previews
- Stack and outcome highlights

### 3. Technical Depth Layer

Purpose:
- Show how Gabriel thinks as a builder.
- Help technical audiences evaluate architecture and engineering decisions.

Expected elements:
- Architecture Lab
- Code philosophy
- Technical decisions
- Engineering principles

### 4. Design and Process Layer

Purpose:
- Show UI/UX sensitivity and product decision-making.
- Demonstrate that design is intentional, not decorative.

Expected elements:
- UI/UX showcase
- Interaction rationale
- Design process notes
- Visual system thinking

### 5. Professional Positioning Layer

Purpose:
- Help recruiters and clients quickly understand fit.
- Translate technical work into employability and market value.

Expected elements:
- Professional timeline
- Market positioning
- Skills map
- Recruiter-friendly summaries

### 6. AI Interaction Layer

Purpose:
- Make the portfolio feel like an operating system.
- Allow visitors to ask questions through trusted portfolio knowledge.

Expected elements:
- AI Agent Console
- Audience modes
- Project explainer flows
- Controlled knowledge boundaries

### 7. Conversion Layer

Purpose:
- Turn interest into contact, opportunity, or deeper engagement.

Expected elements:
- Contact pathways
- Resume / profile access
- Availability signals
- Clear next actions

## Content Architecture Guidelines

The system should separate content into durable domains.

### Structured content domains

- Projects
- Case studies
- Skills
- Timeline entries
- Architecture notes
- Professional summaries
- Market positioning copy
- AI knowledge sources

### Content rules

- Repeated content should live in data files or content modules, not directly inside UI components.
- Content should be reusable across homepage sections, detail pages, and future AI retrieval.
- Each content type should be easy to extend without editing multiple UI files.
- Narrative copy should support both human reading and future AI grounding.

## Product Structure Principles

- Build the portfolio as a system of reusable sections, not one-off pages.
- Optimize for clear visitor progression from first impression to deeper trust.
- Keep architecture simple in early phases, but leave room for future expansion.
- Prefer stable data models over ad hoc content placement.
- Make every major section independently evolvable.
- Ensure the product can support multiple audience perspectives without duplicating the whole site.

## Decision Framework

When reviewing a feature or section, answer these questions:

1. What audience need does this solve?
2. Does this increase trust, clarity, proof, or conversion?
3. Should this be a reusable pattern or a one-time implementation?
4. Does the content belong in UI, data, or future knowledge sources?
5. Will this decision make future AI features easier or harder?
6. Is the experience premium without becoming visually noisy?
7. Is this adding real value or just complexity?

## Rules

- Think product-first, not page-first.
- Keep the structure modular and extensible.
- Separate content, presentation, and behavior.
- Prefer simple systems that can grow over complex systems too early.
- Align every major section with a clear audience need.
- Preserve the cinematic, premium, and technical identity of the portfolio.
- Prioritize evidence of work over self-description.
- Design for future AI retrieval, not only visual presentation.
- Avoid architecture that depends on hardcoded copy inside components.
- Avoid adding sections that do not strengthen proof, clarity, or positioning.

## Output Format

1. Product interpretation
2. Audience goals
3. Information architecture
4. Section strategy
5. Content and data needs
6. Reusability and system patterns
7. Implementation priorities
8. Risks and recommendations
